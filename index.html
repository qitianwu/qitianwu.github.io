Bio

I am a final-year PhD candidate at the Department of Computer Science and Engineering from Shanghai Jiao Tong University (SJTU), advised by Prof. Junchi Yan. I also extensively collaborated with David Wipf and Prof. Hongyuan Zha. I achieved the Bachelor (Microelectronics, Mathematics) and Master (Computer Science) degrees from SJTU, and worked as research intern at Tencent WeChat, Amazon AI Lab and BioMap.

My research interest predominantly revolve around machine learning (ML) foundations and applications. On the foundation side, I build theoretically principled and practically useful methodology, particularly for learning with complex structures that can be observed (e.g., graphs, sequences, etc.) or unobserved (e.g., data manifold geometries). On the application side, I explore the intersection with interdisciplinary areas such as social science, life science and natural science. My research is supported by Microsoft PhD Fellowship and Baidu PhD Fellowship.

I am now on the job market. If you are interested in my research feel free to contact me via email.

Research Summary

My recent works are dedicated to pushing the frontier in machine learning with complex structured data under both closed-world and open-world hypothesis.

  • In the closed-world regime, wherein training and test data are generated from an identical distribution, the research focus is how to build foundation models as general-purpose backbones for obtaining high-quality representation. My recent works particularly focus on the scalability and interpretability, two under-explored aspects concerning building foundation models for data involving complex structures.
    • For scalability, our works (NodeFormer on NeurIPS'22 and SGFormer on NeurIPS'23) explore designing scalable and efficient models that can discover and leverage all-pair global interactions with the cost of linear complexity.
    • For interpretability, our work (DIFFormer on ICLR'23) probes into the analogy between neural message passing and energy-constrained diffusion systems, yielding a physics-inspired interpretable framework for architectural designs.
  • In the open-world regime, wherein training and test data come from disparate distributions, the research focus is how to handle the distribution shifts in data. My recent works endeavor to understand the generalization capability of neural networks and develop generalizable and robust learning algorithms to address distribution shifts.
    • For studying the generalizability, our work (EERM on ICLR'22) formulates the out-of-distribution generalization problem on graphs and proposes a learning algorithm with provable guarantees. The latter is applied to handling distribution shifts in molecular structures (MoleOOD on NeurIPS'22) and user behaviors in recommender systems (Caseq on NeurIPS'22).
    • For enhancing the robustness, our work (GNNSafe on ICLR'23) targets how to endow the pre-trained model with capabilities for identifying data from distributions different than training, i.e., out-of-distribution detection.

Bio

Qitian finished the PhD in Computer Science from Shanghai Jiao Tong University (SJTU), Before that, he achieved the Bachelor (Microelectronics, Mathematics) and Master (Computer Science) degrees from SJTU, and worked as research intern at Tencent WeChat, Amazon Web Service and BioMap.

His research interest predominantly revolves around machine learning foundations and applications. On the foundation side, he builds theoretically principled and practically useful methodology, particularly for learning with complex structures and distribution shifts. He also explores the intersection with interdisciplinary areas such as life sciences (e.g., drug discovery and healthcare) and recommender systems, and sought inspirations from physics. Qitian is the recipients of Microsoft PhD Fellowship, Baidu PhD Fellowship and Academic Scholar Star at SJTU.

Research Summary

My research works aim at machine learning with complex structured data, especially making the models more expressive, efficient and reliable. in both closed-world and open-world regimes.

  • In the closed-world regime, wherein training and test data distributions are overlapped, my research focus is how to build foundation models as general-purpose encoder backbones (e.g., Transformers) for obtaining high-quality representation. My recent works particularly focus on the scalability and interpretability, two key challenges concerning building foundation models for data involving geometries and structures.
    • For scalability, our works (NodeFormer on NeurIPS'22 and SGFormer on NeurIPS'23) explore designing scalable and efficient models that can discover and leverage all-pair global interactions with the cost of linear complexity.
    • For interpretability, our work (DIFFormer on ICLR'23) probes into the analogy between neural message passing and energy-constrained diffusion systems, yielding a physics-inspired interpretable framework for architectural designs.
  • In the open-world regime, wherein training and test data come from disparate distributions, the research focus is how to handle the distribution shifts in the wild. My recent works endeavor to understand the generalization limits of neural networks and develop provably generalizable learning algorithms to address distribution shifts.
    • For studying the generalizability, our work (EERM on ICLR'22) formulates the out-of-distribution generalization problem on graphs and proposes a provably generalizable learning algorithm with invariance principles. From another viewpoint, our work (CaNet on WWW'24) discusses how causal intervention can be utilized for enchaning generalization, and the follow-up work (GLIND on ICML'24) further extends the causal perspective to diffusion systems.
    • For enhancing the robustness, our work (GNNSafe on ICLR'23) targets how to endow the pre-trained model with capabilities for identifying data from distributions different than training, i.e., out-of-distribution detection.

I am always open for collaborations, and if you are interested, feel free to contact me via email.
